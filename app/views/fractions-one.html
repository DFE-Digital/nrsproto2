Using fractions in real life

Take a look at this headline from a press release from the British Wind Energy Association:

“Three-quarters of people in Wales believe wind farms are necessary, says new poll”

Questions

  1. Do the majority of Welsh people think wind farms are necessary?
  2. Do you know how many people think wind farms are necessary?

View the answer to question 1
Yes you know the proportion of people who think they are necessary is a larger part of the total group. Quarters splits something into 4. 3 out of the 4 think they are necessary and 1 doesn’t.
View the answer to question 2
No, you don’t actually know the number of people who think this way – the headline just tells you the proportion in favour, or how many people support wind farms compared with the whole group.
You’ve now found out that 600 people were asked about wind farms
  1. How many people believe wind farms are necessary?
View the answer to question 3
450 people think that wind farms are necessary. That’s 3⁄4 of 600

This starts to show you just what a fraction is – it tells you a proportion rather than what the actual numbers were that enabled this fraction to be written.
